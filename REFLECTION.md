In a bounty system, some information must be 
public so participants can trust the process is 
fair. The bounty title, rubric, reward amount, 
and deadlines should all be visible to everyone 
from the start so participants know exactly what 
they are competing for and under what rules. The 
identity of the winner and the reward payout 
should also be public after judging is complete 
so the outcome can be verified by anyone.

What should stay hidden is each participant's 
actual answer until the submission window is 
closed. If answers are public during submission, 
later participants can copy and improve on earlier 
ideas, which is fundamentally unfair in a 
winner-takes-all bounty. The commit-reveal system 
achieves this by keeping answers hidden behind a 
hash until everyone has locked in their submission.

AI should handle the evaluation and ranking of 
answers because it can apply the rubric 
consistently across all submissions without bias 
or favoritism. Batch judging ensures every answer 
is compared against the same standard in one 
evaluation. However, the final decision of who 
receives the reward should always remain with a 
human because AI output can contain errors and 
the financial consequence of paying out a reward 
requires human accountability before execution.
